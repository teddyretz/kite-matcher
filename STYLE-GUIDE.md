# Ted's Writing Style Guide

## Who this is for

Anyone writing on Ted's behalf — AI tools, editors, collaborators. This covers the Substack and anything adjacent: product copy, social posts, explainers.

---

## The core thing

Write like a smart friend thinking out loud, not a pundit delivering a verdict.

The reader should feel like they're getting a text from someone who actually cares about the subject — not a newsletter from someone building a brand. You can tell which one you're reading in the first two sentences.

---

## Voice

First person. Direct. Opinionated without being performative about it.

Use "I" freely. "I keep coming back to..." or "Here's what gets me..." signals a real person with a real reaction. Neutral reporting is for Wikipedia.

Earn the hot take. Spend real time on the uncomfortable side of the argument — the part that makes the take less clean. Land the view at the end, not the beginning. The reader should feel like you actually wrestled with it.

Never talk down. The audience is non-technical but not unsophisticated. They're smart people who haven't spent years thinking about this specific thing. Write to that, not past it.

---

## Sentence rhythm

Mix long and short. Long sentences when the thought is genuinely tangled or the feeling is anxious or spiraling — let the sentence mirror that. Short ones when something lands.

Run-on sentences are fine when they're doing something. A sentence that keeps going because the thought keeps going is honest. A sentence that keeps going because the writer couldn't stop is a different problem.

One-sentence paragraphs are underused. They work.

---

## What to do

Steelman the other side first. Whatever the opposing view is, take it seriously before you push back. If you can't steelman it, you don't understand it well enough to have a take yet.

Get specific. Not "people are worried about AI" but "my friend who's a paralegal asked me last week if she should be scared." Specificity is what separates thinking from noise.

Have feelings. Not just "this is interesting" — "this is impressive but also kind of unsettling and I'm not totally sure how to hold both of those at once."

Let some mess in. Tangents, half-formed thoughts, genuine uncertainty. Perfect structure feels assembled. Good writing feels discovered.

---

## What not to do

Don't announce what you're about to say. Just say it.

Don't end on vague optimism. "The future is uncertain but exciting" is a nothing-sentence. If you don't know where it lands, say that specifically — "I don't know how this ends" is a real ending.

Don't use em dashes as a personality substitute. One per piece, maybe.

Banned words: *tapestry*, *landscape* (as metaphor), *delve*, *underscore*, *pivotal*, *testament*, *vibrant*, *showcase*, *nuanced* (unless actually drawing a distinction), *foster*, *crucial*, *key* (as adjective).

Don't reach for three things when two will do. "X, Y, and Z" is an AI fingerprint. If it really needs three, fine — but that's not the default.

---

## The north star post

> "AI is very weird for me because normally I'd be the guy who'd argue that it's crazy we're not more excited about this miracle technology, but I completely get this sentiment..."

This is the tone. Notice: it starts mid-thought, it takes the uncomfortable feeling seriously, it's self-aware without undercutting the argument. It doesn't perform depth — it has it.

---

## What this is not

Not a newsletter voice. Not a thought-leader voice. Not a brand.

Someone who thinks about this stuff, has a real take, and doesn't pretend to know more than they do. That's the whole brief.
